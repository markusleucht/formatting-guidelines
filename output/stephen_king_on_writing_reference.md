# Stephen King's On Writing: A Comprehensive Reference Guide

## Overview

"On Writing: A Memoir of the Craft" is part memoir, part master class, offering Stephen King's insights into the art and craft of writing. Published in 2000, the book combines personal narrative with practical advice that has influenced writers across all genres. This reference guide distills the key principles, techniques, and philosophy from King's seminal work.

## Part I: The Writer's Toolkit

### The Essential Tools

King identifies several fundamental tools every writer must master:

#### 1. Vocabulary

- Use the vocabulary you have naturally
- Don't artificially inflate your language with a thesaurus
- Write with the first word that comes to mind, if it's appropriate and colorful
- Avoid fancy words when simple ones will do
- Your natural vocabulary is your authentic voice

**King's Rule:** "Put your vocabulary on the top shelf of your toolbox, and don't make any conscious effort to improve it."

#### 2. Grammar

- Basic grammar is essential but not the whole story
- Grammar is the pole you grab to pull your thoughts up on dry land
- Bad grammar creates confusion and impedes communication
- Good grammar creates clarity without calling attention to itself
- Learn the rules before you break them

**Key Grammar Points:**
- Understand sentence structure (subject-verb-object)
- Know your parts of speech
- Master paragraph construction
- Use proper punctuation to control rhythm and pace

#### 3. Style Elements

**The Adverb Problem:**
- Adverbs are not your friend
- They're like dandelions - one seems harmless, but they multiply
- Particularly avoid adverbs after "he said" or "she said"
- Let the verb and context carry the meaning
- "The road to hell is paved with adverbs"

**Examples to Avoid:**
- "He said angrily" → Show the anger through dialogue and action
- "She ran quickly" → "She sprinted" or "She dashed"
- "The door closed softly" → "The door whispered shut"

**The Passive Voice:**
- Passive voice weakens your writing
- Active voice creates energy and clarity
- Passive: "The meeting will be chaired by me"
- Active: "I will chair the meeting"
- Timid writers like passive voice; it feels safer but less powerful

## Part II: The Writing Process

### Write with the Door Closed, Rewrite with the Door Open

This is one of King's central metaphors for the writing process:

#### First Draft: Door Closed

**Writing for Yourself:**
- Write your first draft with the door closed - just you and the story
- Don't think about audience, critics, or market
- Write at whatever pace feels natural (King suggests 2,000 words/day minimum)
- Let the story tell itself without self-censorship
- This is where you discover what the story is about
- First drafts are for getting it down, not getting it right

**The Process:**
1. Start with a situation, not a plot
2. Let characters drive the story through their choices
3. Write continuously without excessive revision
4. Push through to the end, even when it's difficult
5. Resist the urge to show anyone

#### Second Draft: Door Open

**Writing for Readers:**
- Now invite readers into the process
- This is where you shape and refine
- Cut approximately 10% of your word count
- Look for places where the story drags or confuses
- Strengthen weak sections
- King's formula: "2nd Draft = 1st Draft - 10%"

**What to Look For:**
- Unclear motivations
- Inconsistent characterization
- Weak or confusing passages
- Excessive exposition
- Repetitive phrasing or ideas
- Opportunities to show rather than tell

## Part III: Core Writing Principles

### 1. Truth and Honesty

**Write What You Know:**
- Not literally - you can write about Mars without visiting
- Know the emotions, the human truth
- Draw on real feelings and observations
- Authenticity resonates with readers
- Dishonesty in writing is immediately detectable

**Finding the Truth:**
- Write about real emotions, even in fantastical situations
- Base characters on real psychological observations
- Use specific, concrete details from your life
- Let characters speak and act truthfully, not conveniently

### 2. Description

**King's Approach:**
- Description begins in the writer's imagination and finishes in the reader's
- Don't over-describe - leave room for reader imagination
- Focus on key details that matter
- Use all five senses, not just visual
- Description should advance character or plot

**Three Levels of Description:**
1. **Too Little:** Reader can't visualize anything
2. **Just Right:** A few well-chosen details that activate reader's imagination
3. **Too Much:** Overwhelming catalog that bores the reader

**King's Technique:**
- Lead with a strong visual detail
- Add 2-3 supporting sensory details
- Stop and move on
- Trust the reader to fill in the rest

### 3. Dialogue

**Natural Speech:**
- Dialogue is character
- Listen to how people actually talk
- Include contractions, fragments, interruptions
- Avoid formal or stilted language unless that's the character
- Dialogue should have subtext - what's not said matters

**Dialogue Mechanics:**
- Use "said" as your default dialogue tag
- Avoid elaborate replacements ("he articulated," "she opined")
- Let the dialogue itself convey emotion
- Paragraph breaks show speaker changes
- Don't over-explain with adverbs after "said"

**King's Rules:**
- Keep it brief
- Make it distinctive for each character
- Use it to reveal character
- Advance the plot
- Create rhythm in your prose

### 4. Character

**Character Over Plot:**
- Story comes from character, not the other way around
- Put interesting people in difficult situations
- See what they do
- Don't force characters to serve plot
- Let them make their own (sometimes surprising) choices

**Creating Believable Characters:**
- Give them contradictions
- Base them on real behavioral observations
- Know their backgrounds, even if you don't share it all
- Let them grow and change
- Make them active, not passive

### 5. Show, Don't Tell

**The Fundamental Rule:**
- Don't tell us the moon is shining; show us the glint of light on broken glass
- Actions and images over explanations
- Let readers experience, not just know
- Trust your reader's intelligence

**Examples:**
- **Telling:** "Jane was angry"
- **Showing:** "Jane slammed her coffee mug on the desk, hot liquid sloshing over her papers"

- **Telling:** "The house was old and creepy"
- **Showing:** "Wallpaper peeled in long strips, and each floorboard groaned under my weight"

## Part IV: The Writing Life

### 1. Reading

**Reading as Foundation:**
- "If you don't have time to read, you don't have time to write"
- Read constantly and widely
- Read good books to learn what works
- Read bad books to learn what doesn't
- Reading teaches story structure, rhythm, and technique unconsciously

**What to Read:**
- Read both in and out of your genre
- Read literary fiction for craft
- Read popular fiction for storytelling
- Read non-fiction for ideas and voice
- Read poetry for language and rhythm

### 2. The Writing Schedule

**Consistency Over Inspiration:**
- Write every day, including weekends and holidays
- Set a daily word count goal (King uses 2,000 words)
- Same time, same place when possible
- Routine creates the mental space for creativity
- Waiting for inspiration is procrastination

**King's Daily Practice:**
- Write 2,000 words per day (about 180,000 words/3 months for a novel)
- No excuses except genuine emergencies
- Morning writing when mind is fresh
- Finish first drafts in a single season (3 months maximum)
- Then let it sit for 6 weeks minimum before revision

### 3. The Writing Space

**Create Your Environment:**
- Dedicated space signals your brain: it's writing time
- Door that closes (literally and metaphorically)
- Comfortable but not too comfortable
- Eliminate distractions
- Same space builds habit and focus

### 4. Dealing with Fear and Doubt

**Overcoming Obstacles:**
- Every writer faces fear and doubt
- Perfectionism is the enemy
- First drafts are supposed to be rough
- You can't edit a blank page
- Writing is an act of courage

**King's Experience:**
- Even after decades and bestsellers, doubt persists
- Push through by showing up daily
- Trust the process
- Remember: you're telling a story, not curing cancer
- Take the work seriously, not yourself

## Part V: Revision

### The Revision Process

**Step 1: The Cooling Off Period**
- Finish your first draft completely
- Put it away for at least 6 weeks
- Don't peek, don't tinker
- Work on something else
- Return with fresh eyes

**Step 2: The Read-Through**
- Read your entire manuscript in one or two sittings
- Take notes but don't edit yet
- Look for the big picture: does the story work?
- Identify major problems: plot holes, weak characters, unclear themes
- Trust your instinct - if something feels wrong, it probably is

**Step 3: The Rewrite**
- Apply the "10% rule" - cut 10% of your word count
- Look for:
  - Unnecessary words and phrases
  - Redundant scenes
  - Excessive description
  - Weak adverbs and passive constructions
  - Places where you tell instead of show

**Step 4: Feedback**
- Choose 4-8 trusted readers
- Give them specific questions if helpful
- Listen to their feedback without being defensive
- Look for patterns in what multiple readers say
- Remember: you're not obligated to accept every suggestion

### What to Cut

**Kill Your Darlings:**
- Sometimes your favorite passages must go
- If it doesn't serve the story, cut it
- Clever writing that draws attention to itself
- Beautiful descriptions that slow pacing
- Witty dialogue that doesn't advance character or plot

**Common Cuts:**
- Excessive backstory
- Redundant dialogue tags
- Over-explanation of what's already clear
- Scenes that don't advance the story
- Character thoughts that duplicate action

## Part VI: King's Core Philosophy

### The Writer's Covenant

**Three Obligations:**
1. **To the Story:** Tell it as truthfully as possible
2. **To the Reader:** Be clear and engaging
3. **To Yourself:** Be honest and authentic

### Writing as Telepathy

King's central metaphor: writing is telepathy across time and space.

- You put thoughts and images in your reader's mind
- The reader recreates your vision in their imagination
- This requires clarity, specificity, and honesty
- The magic is in the connection between writer and reader

### The Importance of Theme

**Let Theme Emerge:**
- Don't start with a message or theme
- Write the story first
- Theme reveals itself in revision
- Ask: "What is this about?" beyond the plot
- Theme should be subtle, not preachy

**King's Process:**
- First draft: discovery (what happens)
- Second draft: deepening (what it means)
- Theme emerges from the choices characters make
- Reinforce theme through symbol and repetition
- But never at the expense of story

## Part VII: Practical Advice

### On Plot vs. Situation

**King's Approach:**
- Start with a situation, not a plot
- Plot is artificial; situation is organic
- Ask: "What if?" not "What happens next?"
- Let the story grow from character choices
- Trust the process of discovery

**Example:**
- Situation: A writer is held captive by his "number one fan"
- This became "Misery"
- The plot emerged from what the characters would realistically do
- Not plotted in advance, but discovered through writing

### On Writer's Block

**King's Solution:**
- Writer's block is often fear masquerading as inability
- The cure is to write anyway
- Lower your standards temporarily
- Write garbage if necessary - you can fix it later
- Keep the daily practice no matter what

**Practical Steps:**
1. Show up at your regular time
2. Write anything - journal, letters, exercises
3. Don't judge the quality
4. Trust that the well will refill
5. Remember: you're allowed to write badly

### On Publishing

**The Business Side:**
- Write for yourself first, market second
- Rejection is part of the process (King papered a nail with rejections)
- Persistence matters more than talent
- Don't quit your day job until you can afford to
- The goal is to tell the story, not to be famous

## Part IX: Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Language Mistakes

- **Overwriting:** Using elaborate language when simple words work better
- **Adverb abuse:** Weakening verbs with unnecessary modifiers
- **Passive constructions:** Robbing sentences of energy
- **Cliché:** Using tired phrases instead of fresh language
- **Thesaurus syndrome:** Choosing words for impressiveness rather than accuracy

### 2. Structural Mistakes

- **Excessive backstory:** Dumping information instead of revealing gradually
- **Slow starts:** Taking too long to engage the reader
- **Meandering middles:** Losing focus and momentum
- **Weak endings:** Failing to deliver on the story's promise
- **Unnecessary scenes:** Including material that doesn't advance story or character

### 3. Character Mistakes

- **Passive protagonists:** Characters who observe rather than act
- **Perfect heroes:** Characters without flaws or contradictions
- **Inconsistent behavior:** Characters acting out of character for plot convenience
- **Too many characters:** Confusing readers with unnecessary people
- **Explaining characters:** Telling us who they are instead of showing through action

### 4. Process Mistakes

- **Editing while drafting:** Stopping momentum to perfect each sentence
- **Not finishing:** Starting project after project without completion
- **Skipping the cooling off period:** Revising before you can see clearly
- **Ignoring all feedback:** Being too precious about your work
- **Over-revising:** Polishing the life out of your prose

## Part X: The Toolbox Metaphor

King uses the metaphor of a toolbox with different levels:

### Top Shelf: Vocabulary
- Your natural word choice
- Don't artificially inflate it
- Use what you know

### Second Shelf: Grammar
- The basic rules
- Structure and clarity
- Foundation for everything else

### Third Shelf: Elements of Style
- Active voice
- Avoiding adverbs
- Show don't tell
- Paragraph and sentence rhythm

### Bottom Shelf: Everything Else
- Plot (or situation)
- Character
- Theme
- Symbolism
- All the complex craft elements

The key: master the top shelves before worrying about the bottom. Strong vocabulary, solid grammar, and clean style will carry you far even before you tackle the more complex elements.

## Part XI: Exercises and Practices

While King doesn't provide formal exercises, these practices emerge from his advice:

### Daily Writing Practice
1. Write at the same time every day
2. Set a word count goal (start with 1,000, work up to 2,000)
3. Complete your goal before stopping
4. Track your progress

### Reading Practice
1. Read for at least 4-5 hours daily (King's habit)
2. Analyze what works in books you love
3. Identify what doesn't work in books you don't
4. Read actively, thinking about craft choices

### Revision Practice
1. Print your work (seeing it in new format helps)
2. Read with a red pen
3. Cut 10% ruthlessly
4. Circle every adverb - remove 90% of them
5. Highlight passive voice - convert to active
6. Mark places where you tell instead of show

### Description Practice
1. Describe a room in 3-4 sentences
2. Describe a person with 2-3 key details
3. Practice using all five senses
4. Practice specific rather than general details

## Part XII: Application Beyond Fiction

While "On Writing" focuses on fiction, the principles apply broadly:

### For Business Writing
- Clarity and simplicity trump elaborate language
- Active voice creates energy and accountability
- Cut ruthlessly - respect your reader's time
- Show with data and examples, don't just assert
- Read widely to absorb good communication patterns

### For Academic Writing
- Honest voice even within formal constraints
- Clear thinking produces clear writing
- Cut jargon that doesn't add meaning
- Trust your reader's intelligence
- Revision is where real thinking happens

### For Personal Writing
- Authenticity connects with readers
- Write regularly to maintain the practice
- Read to see what's possible
- Don't wait for perfect conditions
- The first draft is for discovery

---

## Conclusion: The Heart of King's Message

At its core, "On Writing" delivers a simple but profound message:

1. **Write honestly** - Truth resonates with readers
2. **Write regularly** - Daily practice builds skill and discipline
3. **Write simply** - Clear, direct prose is powerful prose
4. **Write courageously** - Fear is normal; write anyway
5. **Write humbly** - Be willing to revise and improve

King demystifies writing while respecting its difficulty. He offers no shortcuts, no tricks, no easy paths. Just the tools, the discipline, and the encouragement to do the work.

The best part? Anyone can do it. You don't need special talent or perfect conditions. You need commitment, honesty, and the courage to keep showing up at the page.

As King himself puts it: "You can, you should, and if you're brave enough to start, you will."
